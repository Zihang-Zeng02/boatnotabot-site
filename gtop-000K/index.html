Definition. Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra [gtop-000K]

  • March 10, 2026
  • Zihang Zeng

Let \((X, \tau )\) be a topological space. The Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra \(\mathcal {B}(X)\) is the smallest \(\sigma \)-algebra on \(X\) containing all open sets.

Since \(\mathcal {B}(X)\) is generated by the open sets, it also contains all closed sets, countable unions of closed sets (\(F_\sigma \) sets), countable intersections of open sets (\(G_\delta \) sets), and so on.

    Intuition. Intuition: Borel Measure [todo-0006]

    • March 11, 2026
    • Zihang Zeng

    A Borel measure assigns "sizes" (lengths, areas, volumes, probabilities) to sets built from open sets.

    The topology tells you what is open, the Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra tells you what is measurable, and a Borel measure tells you how big. It is a function that assigns a non-negative size to each Borel set, respecting the rule that the measure of a countable disjoint union equals the sum of the measures.

    Why not measure all subsets? In spaces like \(\mathbb {R}\), there exist pathological sets (via the axiom of choice) that cannot be assigned a size consistently. The Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra avoids this by only including sets constructible from open sets through countable operations — which turns out to be rich enough for essentially all practical purposes.

    Intuition. Intuition: Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra [todo-0005]

    • March 11, 2026
    • Zihang Zeng

    The Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra is what you get by starting from the open sets and asking: what is the smallest \(\sigma \)-algebra containing all of them?

    The topology decides what is open. The Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra is the cheapest way to turn that into something you can measure. You take the open sets and "complete" them: add all closed sets (complements), countable intersections of open sets (\(G_\delta \) sets), countable unions of closed sets (\(F_\sigma \) sets), and so on.

    Why minimal? A bigger \(\sigma \)-algebra means more sets to measure, which is harder to do consistently. The Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra includes exactly the sets that the topology forces you to measure — nothing more.

    Intuition. Intuition: Measure vs Metric [todo-0003]

    • March 11, 2026
    • Zihang Zeng

    A measure assigns a "size" to a set — how much stuff is in it. Length, area, volume, and probability are all measures.

    A metric assigns a "distance" between two points — how far apart they are.

    They answer fundamentally different questions: measure asks "how big is this region?" while metric asks "how far apart are these two points?"

    A metric is upstream of measure: a metric defines open balls, which give a topology, which give a Borel \(\sigma \)-algebra, which gives sets you can measure. A set can be small in measure but wide in metric (e.g., a thin line segment across a room).

      Definition. \(\sigma \)-algebra [gtop-000M]

      • March 10, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      A \(\sigma \)-algebra on a set \(X\) is a collection \(\mathcal {F} \subseteq \mathcal {P}(X)\) of subsets of \(X\) satisfying:

      1. \(X \in \mathcal {F}\).
      2. If \(A \in \mathcal {F}\), then \(X \setminus A \in \mathcal {F}\) (closed under complements).
      3. If \(\left \{ A_n \right \}_{n=1}^{\infty } \subseteq \mathcal {F}\), then \(\bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty } A_n \in \mathcal {F}\) (closed under countable unions).

      The pair \((X, \mathcal {F})\) is called a measurable space, and the members of \(\mathcal {F}\) are called measurable sets.

      Definition. Closed Set [gtop-0006]

      • March 7, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      Let \((X, \tau )\) be a topological space. A subset \(C \subseteq X\) is called closed if its complement \(X \setminus C\) is open, i.e. \(X \setminus C \in \tau \).

      Equivalently, the collection of closed sets satisfies:

      1. \(\varnothing \) and \(X\) are closed.
      2. Arbitrary intersections of closed sets are closed.
      3. Finite unions of closed sets are closed.

      Theorem. Closed Set Characterization in Metric Spaces [gtop-000U]

      • March 11, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      Let \((X, d)\) be a metric space and \(C \subseteq X\). Then \(C\) is closed if and only if \(C\) contains all its limit points.

      Equivalently, \(C\) is closed if and only if for every convergent sequence \(\left \{ x_n \right \} \subseteq C\) with \(x_n \to x\), we have \(x \in C\).

      This characterization relies on the metric structure. In a general topological space, sequences may not suffice to detect all limit points.

      Definition. Open Set [gtop-0005]

      • March 7, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      Let \((X, \tau )\) be a topological space. A subset \(U \subseteq X\) is called open if \(U \in \tau \).

      In a Euclidean space \(\mathbb {R}^n\), a subset \(U \subseteq \mathbb {R}^n\) is open if for every \(x \in U\), there exists \(\epsilon > 0\) such that \(B(x, \epsilon ) \subseteq U\). More generally, in any metric space \((X, d)\), a set is open if it is a union of open balls.

      The Euclidean definition is a special case where the topology is induced by the metric \(d(x, y) = \lVert x - y \rVert \).

      Definition. Topological Space [gtop-0002]

      • March 7, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      A topological space is a pair \((X, \tau )\) where \(X\) is a set and \(\tau \subseteq \mathcal {P}(X)\) is a collection of subsets of \(X\), called a topology on \(X\), satisfying:

      1. \(\varnothing , X \in \tau \)
      2. If \(\left \{ U_\alpha \right \}_{\alpha \in A} \subseteq \tau \), then \(\bigcup _{\alpha \in A} U_\alpha \in \tau \) (closed under arbitrary unions).
      3. If \(U_1, U_2 \in \tau \), then \(U_1 \cap U_2 \in \tau \) (closed under finite intersections).

      The elements of \(\tau \) are called open sets. A set \(C \subseteq X\) is called closed if its complement \(X \setminus C\) is open.

      Example. Topologies on a Three-Point Set [gtop-000P]

      • March 11, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      Let \(X = \left \{ 1, 2, 3 \right \}\). The following are all valid topologies on \(X\):

      • Indiscrete: \(\tau _1 = \left \{ \varnothing , X \right \}\). Only \(\varnothing \) and \(X\) are open.
      • Partial: \(\tau _2 = \left \{ \varnothing , \left \{ 1 \right \}, X \right \}\). Only \(\left \{ 1 \right \}\) and the trivial sets are open.
      • Discrete: \(\tau _3 = \mathcal {P}(X)\). Every subset is open.

      Each satisfies the three axioms of a topological space. The same set \(X\) carries different topological structure depending on the choice of \(\tau \).

      Example. Open, Closed, Both, or Neither [gtop-000Q]

      • March 11, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      Let \(X = \left \{ 1,2,3 \right \}\) with \(\tau = \left \{ \varnothing , \left \{ 1 \right \}, X \right \}\). A subset need not be open or closed — it can be both, or neither.

      • \(\left \{ 1 \right \}\) is open (in \(\tau \)), but not closed (\(X \setminus \left \{ 1 \right \} = \left \{ 2,3 \right \} \notin \tau \)).
      • \(\left \{ 2,3 \right \}\) is closed (\(X \setminus \left \{ 2,3 \right \} = \left \{ 1 \right \} \in \tau \)), but not open.
      • \(\varnothing \) and \(X\) are both open and closed.
      • \(\left \{ 1,2 \right \}\) is neither open (not in \(\tau \)) nor closed (\(X \setminus \left \{ 1,2 \right \} = \left \{ 3 \right \} \notin \tau \)).
