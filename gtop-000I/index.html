Definition. Interior Point [gtop-000I]

  • March 11, 2026
  • Zihang Zeng

If \(S\) is a subset of a Euclidean space, then \(x\) is an interior point of \(S\) if there exists an open ball centered at \(x\) which is completely contained in \(S\).

    Definition. Interior of a Set [gtop-000J]

    • March 11, 2026
    • Zihang Zeng

    The interior of a subset \(S\) of a topological space \(X\), denoted by \(\operatorname {int}_X\, S\) or \(\operatorname {int}\, S\) or \(S^{\circ }\), can be defined in any of the following equivalent ways:

    1. \(\operatorname {int}\, S\) is the largest open subset of \(X\) contained in \(S\).
    2. \(\operatorname {int}\, S\) is the union of all open sets of \(X\) contained in \(S\).
    3. \(\operatorname {int}\, S\) is the set of all interior points of \(S\).

      Definition. Euclidean Space [gtop-0004]

      • March 7, 2026
      • Zihang Zeng

      The Euclidean space \(\mathbb {R}^n\) is the metric space equipped with the Euclidean metric

      \[d(x, y) = \lVert x - y \rVert = \sqrt {\sum _{i=1}^{n} (x_i - y_i)^2}\]

      The topology induced by this metric is called the standard topology on \(\mathbb {R}^n\). In this topology, the open ball centered at \(x\) with radius \(\epsilon > 0\) is

      \[B(x, \epsilon ) = \left \{ y \in \mathbb {R}^n : \lVert y - x \rVert < \epsilon \right \}\]

      This is a special case of a metric space, which in turn induces a topological space.
